## Position tracking algorithm

**Recommended approach.** Maintain position with a **forward-only local aligner**, not with full-book fuzzy search. Preprocess the book into normalized tokens and preserve original word indices. Normalize ASR output the same way: lowercase, strip punctuation, collapse whitespace, normalize apostrophes, and convert obvious numeral/word variants if your title set needs it. Then keep two token streams: **stable tokens** that have survived finalization or repeated partial agreement, and **volatile tokens** from the latest partial. Only the stable stream should be allowed to move the public cursor. This is exactly the framing that reading-tutor systems use: align imperfect ASR output against known text and score the tracking error in text space over time. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view1ţłéturn6view2ţłéturn7view0ţłü

**Algorithm.** I would use a **two-stage matcher**. Stage one is a cheap candidate generator inside a narrow forward corridor, ideally by indexing the bookÔÇÖs token 2-grams and 3-grams so a short ASR history can propose likely local starts. Stage two is a **weighted monotonic dynamic-programming alignment** between the last 6ÔÇô12 stable ASR tokens and candidate book spans within the corridor. Rasmussen et al. are the right precedent: their reading-tutor tracker used dynamic programming and explicitly assigned low cost to advancing one word, tiny cost to staying put, and high cost to jumps; they also studied a skip-one left-to-right variant because allowing isolated skips can prevent the tracker from getting stuck. Other reading-tutor work improved robustness with phone-level similarity and an HMM-state fallback. That is exactly the design tension your app has: you need a strict left-to-right prior, but you must not get stranded forever after one bad recognition burst. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view1ţłéturn5search7ţłéturn6view2ţłü

**Window size.** My starting implementation constants would be a **normal forward corridor of about 120 words**, an **emergency reacquisition corridor of 250ÔÇô300 words**, and at most **8 words of backward tolerance for matching only** so immediate rereads do not destroy local scoring. Those numbers are recommendations, not claims from the literature, but they fit the actual failure shape you described: you need enough lookahead to recover from being a page or two behind, but not so much that every common phrase appears everywhere and makes the search ambiguous. The cursor still never moves backward; the backward tolerance is only for local scoring and stable-prefix handling. The prior-art support here is the reading-tutor idea that monotonicity should dominate but limited skips/jumps must exist to keep the tracker alive. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view1ţłéturn5search7ţłü

**Do not use Fuse.js as the main tracker.** Fuse.js is useful, but it is the wrong primitive for this job. Its Bitap-based fuzzy matching is optimized for short patterns, has a 32-character per-term limit before splitting, and its scoring is designed around ranked search with configurable location/distance heuristics. That is excellent for ÔÇťfind this short phrase in a field,ÔÇŁ but not for ÔÇťfollow a moving reader through a known text with monotonic prior and off-task speech.ÔÇŁ If you want JS/TS utilities, use `fastest-levenshtein` or `js-levenshtein` for short-string scoring or trigger-phrase fuzziness, and write the actual forward token-DP yourself. ţłÇciteţłéturn39view1ţłéturn39view0ţłéturn39view2ţłéturn8search0ţłéturn8search1ţłü

**Handling rereads, pauses, interruptions, and garbage.** The CMU real-time work is particularly valuable here because it shows that partial hypotheses are not stable; they go through a recognizable cycle of early detection, hallucination, premature recognition, procrastination, and later correction. It also reports substantial off-task speech in oral-reading data. In practice, that means you should not advance the cursor simply because the current partial produced *some* plausible local match. Advance only when the best local alignment is clearly better than the runner-up and remains consistent across two updates. When there is silence or low-confidence garbage, hold position. The ÔÇťcleverÔÇŁ alternativeÔÇöslow synthetic driftÔÇöwill corrupt trigger eligibility and create page-late sounds. ţłÇciteţłéturn7view0ţłéturn6view0ţłü

**Thresholds.** I would begin with these implementation defaults: require at least **5 aligned tokens** before a cursor move can commit; require a **normalized alignment similarity around 0.7 or better**; and require the best candidate to beat the second-best by at least **0.1** before you snap forward. If the platform provides word confidence/timing on the on-device path, blend that into the score as a secondary signal instead of using it alone. The main risk in this conservative setup is that very short pages or one-line spreads may feel sticky. The fallback is to allow a secondary commit rule for short-page templates that relies more heavily on timing/confidence and less on chunk length. ţłÇciteţłéturn41view0ţłéturn41view3ţłéturn6view1ţłéturn7view0ţłü

## Trigger firing precision

**Recommended approach.** Trigger detection should run only inside an **armed corridor**, not against the full rolling transcript. Each trigger should live in a small state machine: `pending -> armed -> fired | expired`. A trigger becomes armed when the public cursor approaches its indexed region, and once armed it is matched only against a short recent token window, usually the last 6ÔÇô10 stable tokens plus the latest volatile suffix. This is how you keep twelve different occurrences of ÔÇťgoodnightÔÇŁ from all competing globally. The trackerÔÇÖs job is to narrow the space; the trigger engineÔÇÖs job is to be precise inside that narrowed space. ţłÇciteţłéturn41view2ţłéturn6view2ţłü

**Multi-word and single-word triggers should not be treated the same.** Multi-word phrases like ÔÇťgoodnight moonÔÇŁ are much more discriminative, so I would allow them a small ordered-edit tolerance: one token deletion/substitution for very short phrases, possibly two for longer ones. Single-word triggers should be much stricter: narrower armed corridor, higher confidence threshold, and usually either an exact normalized match or repeated evidence across two partial frames if the word is common or short. On the supported newer on-device paths, `expo-speech-recognition` can expose word confidence and timing, and I would absolutely use that metadata to tighten single-word decisions. ţłÇciteţłéturn41view0ţłéturn41view3ţłü

**Expiry window.** My starting rule would be: arm a trigger when `cursor >= wordIndex - 12`; expire **single-word** triggers around `wordIndex + 10`; expire **multi-word** triggers around `wordIndex + 18`; and never let expiry drift past about **25 words** unless logs prove a given title needs it. Those are opinionated starting values, not universal constants, but they fit the product better than a generic long timeout. In your UX, a slightly missed effect is less damaging than a stale effect firing a page later. The main risk is expiring too aggressively on slow, expressive readers; the fallback is per-trigger-class expiry tuning based on logged sessions. ţłÇfileciteţłéturn0file0ţłü

**Preventing double-fires and wrong-occurrence fires.** Once a trigger fires, it should enter a brief cooldownÔÇöabout **1.5 seconds or 4ÔÇô6 cursor words**, whichever ends laterÔÇöand then become permanently done for the session. If the same phrase appears again near another trigger later in the book, only the **nearest armed pending trigger** should be eligible. If phrase overlap becomes troublesome, strengthen the rule from ÔÇťphrase detected after wordIndexÔÇŁ to ÔÇťphrase detected while this trigger is the best local armed candidate.ÔÇŁ That shifts authority from the phrase itself to the local position estimate, which is where it belongs in a repeated-phrase book. The conceptual support again comes from reading-tutor systems: position should disambiguate recognition, not the reverse. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view1ţłéturn6view2ţłü

## Latency and audio sync

**Where latency actually comes from.** Your string matcher is not the long pole. The real latency budget is eaten by acoustic buffering, VAD/endpointer behavior, recognizer segmentation, waiting for final results instead of using stable partials, and loading audio at play time. CMUÔÇÖs real-time oral-reading work is explicit about the fundamental tradeoff: more evidence improves correctness, but only after lag has already been introduced. So the wrong architecture is ÔÇťwait until the recognizer is sure, then fire.ÔÇŁ The right architecture is ÔÇťfire when the system is **stable enough** and the trigger is already armed.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn7view0ţłéturn6view0ţłü

**Recommended latency strategy.** Work backward from the UX target. That means: use streaming/interim recognition, avoid end-of-utterance blocking, arm triggers before the indexed phrase, and fire off the first stable local match rather than waiting for a perfect final transcript. On Android continuous mode, remember that results arrive segment by segment and partial/final outputs for new segments must be concatenated. ## Robustness and fallback behavior

**Recommended hold behavior.** When confidence drops, **freeze the public cursor**. Do not slow-advance, do not interpolate, and do not ÔÇťguess throughÔÇŁ silence. The reading-tutor papers make clear why: oral-reading streams contain unstable partial hypotheses and meaningful amounts of off-task speech, and false rejection/false correction erodes trust quickly. In your product, low-confidence forward drift is worse than a temporary freeze because it silently makes stale triggers eligible. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view0ţłéturn7view0ţłéturn37view0ţłü

**Automatic recovery.** Add an explicit **reacquisition mode**. If VAD says speech is ongoing but you have not committed a confident cursor move for roughly **6ÔÇô8 seconds**, temporarily widen the corridor, lengthen the ASR history chunk you are matching, and suppress non-ambient trigger firing until you re-lock. This is where the reading-tutor prior art is useful: limited skip/jump allowance helps prevent deadlock, and phone-based or alternative fallback scoring can help when exact lexical matching fails. ## Recommended architecture

**The architecture I would build.** Use a **streaming recognizer + VAD** that emits timestamps and partial text, a **normalization layer** that turns recognizer output into stable and volatile tokens, a **forward-only local aligner** that maintains both a public cursor and a private shadow cursor, a **trigger engine** that arms only nearby pending triggers by index corridor, and an **audio layer** that preloads every effect for the session and plays one-shots immediately. **Cadence.** Run VAD/audio capture on small native frames, roughly **20ÔÇô30 ms**.
